Educational Philosophy

The values and beliefs that drive my practice.

The Living Philosophy

My philosophy of adult education has developed through a combination of experience, reflection, and disruption. While I value practical, experience-based learning, my understanding of teaching and learning has not developed in a straightforward or consistent way. Instead, it has required me to continually question my assumptions and adapt my practice. I use the metaphor of building a house to represent this process. The foundation reflects my core beliefs about adult learning, while the structure represents the skills and knowledge I have developed over time. However, like any structure, it has required renovation. Changes in my personal and professional life have challenged my assumptions and pushed me to reconsider how I support learners. This ongoing process of building and rebuilding continues to shape my identity as an adult educator


My beliefs about adult learning are grounded in the idea that learning is most effective when it is relevant, applied, and connected to the learner’s prior experience. I view adult learners as capable and resourceful individuals who bring valuable knowledge into the learning environment, but who may require guidance in developing confidence and independence in their learning processes. As an educator, I see my role not as providing answers, but as creating conditions where learners can engage with problems, test ideas, and develop practical solutions. This perspective is influenced by experiential and self-directed approaches to learning, where the goal is not only to acquire knowledge, but to develop the ability to apply that knowledge in meaningful ways. At the same time, I recognize that learners require varying levels of structure and support, which challenges me to balance guidance with autonomy in my practice.


Despite my commitment to applied, learner-centered approaches, I recognize several tensions within my practice. I encourage learners to be independent problem-solvers, yet I also see that some require more direct guidance before they are ready to work autonomously. Similarly, while I value hands-on, experiential learning, the constraints of structured programs and limited time can lead me to rely more on explanation than application. There is also tension between my belief in flexible, learner-driven education and the realities of institutional expectations that shape how and when learning occurs. These tensions highlight that my philosophy is not fully resolved, but instead requires ongoing reflection and adjustment to better align my beliefs with my practice.

Ethical Commitments

Ethical considerations play an important role in shaping my practice as an adult educator, particularly within the context of technology-based learning. I am mindful of the balance between supporting learner independence and providing appropriate guidance, ensuring that learners are challenged without being discouraged. In my teaching, I emphasize the ethical use of technology, including the responsible use of tools such as artificial intelligence, encouraging learners to use these tools to support their understanding rather than replace their thinking. I also recognize my responsibility to create a learning environment that is fair, inclusive, and respectful of diverse experiences and starting points. This requires ongoing reflection on my own assumptions and practices to ensure that I am not unintentionally creating barriers to learning. As a result, ethical practice in my work is not fixed, but something I continually evaluate and refine.
